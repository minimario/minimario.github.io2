spontaneity

I really like having control over my life. I like going into an activity with an idea of how pleasant it will be, what I might get from it, etc. But I also love spontaneity. I like randomly veering from my scheduled day and start doing things I never would have expected to. These seem like they conflict. I love exotic adventures, but I also love order in my life. How can I resolve this paradox?

My theory for why this occurs is as follows. While I can control a lot of our life (lol do I believe in this?), one thing that is hard to predict and control is my feelings. I can’t know how exactly I’ll be feeling in the next 3 hours. I might have an idea that overall, I’ll be feeling pretty happy or pretty sad, but I won’t have control over some of the more spontaneous feeling that arise. While I can’t speak for others’ experiences, I often get an urge to just go do something adventurous or do something relaxing or do something original, and this feeling seemingly pops out of nowhere. And after I do what my brain is telling me to do, I feel thrilled and fulfilled. Way more thrilled and fulfilled than if I had planned to do something and was anticipating it.

There’s a niceness to spontaneity. It’s as if my mind is asking for something and I’m giving it exactly what it wants instantly. And then it feels really satisfied. Sometimes, I wish I could plan for this spontaneity, since it would give my life more order. Which is also nice because certainty suddenly arises, and certainty is nice. There are times where I disappoint my spontaneous mind: like if I’m craving a nice walk outside, but nobody’s willing to go, or if I want to do something outdoors, but it suddenly starts raining. It would certainly have been nicer if I had asked some people earlier or checked the weather to make sure. But it’s almost impossible for me to be able to predict that on a given day, in a few hours, I’ll be in the mood to do X activity.

My mind likes this element of surprise. There’s probably some dopamine release that is connected to the anticipation of how I will feel in a few hours, and then some excitement connected to actually resolving this suspense and discovering what my feelings are, and then even more fulfillment occurring when I satisfy my feelings.

This aspect of finding that surprise motivates me has also allowed me to get myself to do other things. I’m very motivated to do things like experiencing rejection via absurdities. If I knew exactly how I would be rejected and how I would feel, I would be so much less motivated. But it’s the combination of possibly not being rejected, discovering a new data point in my life, and discovering how I’ll feel when I actually do something embarrassing/out of my comfort zone that makes it so thrilling to do something like this. It’s also partially why self-help is so appealing to me. Because I have no idea how the brain works, and I can have a desired change that I want to make, but I’ll suddenly change in so many other ways that I couldn’t have possibly intended. Combined with the aspect that self-help is injecting additional data in my life and learning more about myself, that element of surprise is extremely fulfilling. It’s also one of my motivations for meditation, as I’ll never know what kind of thoughts pop up while I’m in the middle of a meditation session.

I also love novelty and exploration, a different facet of surprise. Exploring neighborhoods and cities, exploring myself, others, exploring how I feel while doing various activities, and so on. There’s also a strange phenomenon where uniqueness that is not surprising feels (at least, to me) the exact same way surprise would. Things like walking on a frozen river are unique and feel the exact same as these spontaneous surprises, but I don’t find these experiences surprising at all. I know (at least, I think I know) what happens when I walk on a frozen river: it’s no different from walking on an ice rink.

But who knows. Maybe all of this is absurd and is just an explanation I’ve given to myself that seems to make sense. And I like when I can explain spontaneity away with reason. However, I can’t. And therefore, I’ve learned to embrace that feelings just arise and pop up for no reason. That some things are rather explained by “it’s just a feeling.” Well, reasons that I don’t know and won’t be able to know.
